The Architect Role

Before you can become an architect, you need to understand what architects actually do — and what separates a great one from a good one.

🏛️ What Architects Actually Do

"Architecture is the decisions you wish you could get right early in a project — decisions that are hard to change later and that have a significant impact on quality and cost."

— Martin Fowler

The Real Job Description (not the one on LinkedIn)

🔭
Define the Vision
Translate business goals into technical strategy. Decide what gets built, when, and how — not just how to build the current ticket.
⚖️
Make Trade-off Decisions
Every architectural choice gives you something and costs you something. Architects own the trade-off decisions that affect the entire system.
🛡️
Guard Quality Attributes
Ensure the system meets non-functional requirements: performance, security, scalability, reliability, maintainability. These don't appear in user stories.
🔗
Reduce Accidental Complexity
Distinguish essential complexity (comes from the problem) from accidental complexity (introduced by poor design). Eliminate the accidental kind ruthlessly.
🧭
Enable the Team
Create guardrails, not walls. Establish principles and standards that let developers make good decisions independently without constant oversight.
📢
Communicate Decisions
A great architecture that nobody understands is useless. Architects spend 40-60% of their time communicating — explaining, writing, presenting, aligning.

What Architects Do NOT Do (common misconceptions)

❌ Myth
  • Write all the hard code
  • Make every technical decision
  • Produce giant upfront design documents
  • Stay isolated from the codebase
  • Only talk to managers and stakeholders
  • Pick technologies because they're new/trendy
✅ Reality
  • Write just enough code to stay credible and informed
  • Delegate decisions to the right level; decide the hard ones
  • Produce lightweight, living decision records (ADRs)
  • Deeply understand the codebase; refactor as needed
  • Spend significant time pairing with developers
  • Pick proven technologies that fit the problem + team

🚀 The Career Journey — Junior to Architect

💡
There's no certification or single event that makes you an architect. It's a gradual expansion of scope, influence, and responsibility. The title follows the behavior.
J
Junior Developer 0–2 yrs
Focus: Write correct code. Understand the tools, frameworks, and codebase. Ask good questions. Learn debugging, testing, git workflows. Your scope is one task.

Key shift to make: Stop thinking "does this work?" → Start thinking "is this the right approach?"
M
Mid-Level Developer 2–5 yrs
Focus: Own features end-to-end. Understand the domain deeply. Review others' code. Know why things work the way they do — not just that they do. Your scope is one feature.

Key shift to make: Stop thinking in single features → Start thinking in modules and contracts between them.
S
Senior Developer 5–8 yrs
Focus: Own a service or domain. Make design decisions for your team. Mentor others. Identify systemic problems before they become production incidents. Your scope is one service/domain.

Key shift to make: Stop thinking in code → Start thinking in systems, teams, and time. A senior dev optimizes code; an architect optimizes the conditions under which good code gets written.
St
Staff Engineer / Tech Lead 7–12 yrs
Focus: Define technical direction across multiple teams or a large system. Drive organizational technical decisions. Write proposals, RFCs, and technical strategies. Your scope is a product or platform.

Key shift to make: Stop thinking in one system → Start thinking in systems of systems, organizational capability, and multi-year evolution.
A
Software Architect / Principal Engineer 10+ yrs
Focus: Define the technical vision for an organization. Make decisions that shape teams, products, and platforms for years. Balance technical excellence with business goals, budget constraints, and team capabilities. Your scope is the entire engineering organization.

What sets you apart: You see the whole board. You think in trade-offs across dimensions most engineers haven't considered yet: team topology, Conway's Law, technical debt economics, build vs buy decisions, platform vs product thinking.

System Thinking

Architects think in systems, not files. This section builds the mental models for reasoning about large-scale systems.

📐 Core Architecture Principles

⚠️
These principles are not rules. They are lenses. You apply them to understand a situation better, not to auto-generate a solution. Knowing when to violate a principle is as important as knowing the principle.

The Big 8 Principles Every Architect Must Internalize

PrincipleWhat It Means in PracticeThe Violation Warning Sign
Separation of Concerns Each component does one well-defined job. UI doesn't know about DB. Business logic doesn't know about HTTP. A 2000-line controller class that validates, transforms, queries, and renders
Single Responsibility A module has one reason to change — one stakeholder who "owns" that logic. Changing a billing rule requires editing the same class as changing email templates
Open/Closed Open for extension (add new behavior), closed for modification (don't edit working code). Adding a new payment type requires editing 5 existing switch/if-else blocks
Dependency Inversion Depend on abstractions, not concretions. Your service calls interface PaymentGateway, not class StripeClient. Swapping from Stripe to Braintree requires rewriting 30 service classes
Favour Composition Build behavior by combining simple objects, not by building deep inheritance hierarchies. A 6-level class hierarchy where changing a base class breaks 20 subclasses
Least Privilege Every component gets only the access it needs. Databases, APIs, services, and users alike. Your microservice has root DB access "just in case we need it later"
Design for Failure Assume every dependency will fail. Design retry logic, circuit breakers, and graceful degradation from the start. A single third-party API timeout takes down your entire checkout flow
Evolutionary Architecture Design systems that can change incrementally without big-bang rewrites. Architecture is not a one-time decision. "We can't change this because it would break everything" — said about a 2-year-old system

Conway's Law — The Most Underestimated Principle

"Organizations which design systems are constrained to produce designs which are copies of the communication structures of those organizations."

— Melvin Conway, 1967

In plain terms: your architecture will mirror your org chart whether you want it to or not. If you have 3 frontend teams and 2 backend teams, you'll end up with 3 frontends talking to 2 backends.

🎯
Inverse Conway Maneuver: Deliberately design your team structure to produce the architecture you want. Want microservices? Create small, independent teams with end-to-end ownership. Want a monolith that stays clean? Keep your team tightly coordinated.

Practical implication: Before you design a system, look at your org chart. The architecture that fights the org chart will always lose to the one that embraces it.

📏 Quality Attributes (Non-Functional Requirements)

💡
Functional requirements say what the system does. Quality attributes say how well it does it. Architects spend most of their time on quality attributes — they're what defines an architecture, not the features.

The Critical Quality Attributes — Every Architect Must Know These Cold

AttributeDefinitionHow to Design For ItKey Metric
Availability System is operational and accessible when users need it Redundancy, failover, health checks, graceful degradation 99.9% = 8.7h/yr downtime; 99.99% = 52m/yr
Scalability Handle growing load without architectural changes Stateless services, horizontal scaling, partitioning/sharding Throughput at N × load; latency at P99
Performance System responds to requests within acceptable time Caching, async processing, indexing, CDN, connection pooling P50 / P95 / P99 latency in ms
Reliability System performs correctly under expected and unexpected conditions Circuit breakers, retries with backoff, idempotency, chaos testing MTBF, error rate %, SLO compliance
Security Protection from unauthorized access, data breaches, abuse Zero trust, least privilege, encryption in transit/at rest, input validation CVSS score, time-to-patch, audit coverage
Maintainability Ease of modifying, debugging, and extending the system Clean code, modularity, good tests, documentation, automated linting Time to onboard new dev, DORA metrics
Observability Ability to understand internal system state from external outputs Structured logging, distributed tracing, metrics, dashboards, alerts Mean time to detect (MTTD)
Testability Ease of verifying system behavior through automated tests Dependency injection, clean interfaces, ports & adapters, seam insertion Test coverage %, mutation score
Deployability Frequency and ease of safely deploying changes CI/CD pipelines, feature flags, blue-green/canary deployments, rollbacks Deployment frequency, lead time (DORA)
Interoperability Ability to work with other systems, standards, and protocols Standard APIs (REST/gRPC), event contracts, API versioning, anti-corruption layers Integration test coverage, API contract tests

🔑 The CAP Theorem — The Architect's Fundamental Trade-off

In a distributed system that can experience network partitions, you can only guarantee 2 of 3: Consistency (all nodes see the same data at the same time), Availability (every request gets a response), Partition Tolerance (system works despite network failures). Since network partitions are inevitable, you're always choosing: CP (prioritize consistency — bank transactions) or AP (prioritize availability — social feeds, shopping carts). Most modern databases make this choice for you: PostgreSQL=CP, Cassandra=AP, DynamoDB=configurable.

🧩 Architectural Patterns

🔄
CQRS
Command Query Responsibility Segregation. Separate the write model (commands that change state) from the read model (queries that return data). Allows each to be optimized independently. Common pattern: write to normalized SQL, read from a denormalized cache/view.
Read/Write splitPerformance
📖
Event Sourcing
Store state as a sequence of events, not as current values. The current state is derived by replaying events. Gives you a full audit log, easy temporal queries, and ability to rebuild any past state. Used in financial systems, booking systems.
Audit trailTime travel
🔁
Saga Pattern
Manage distributed transactions across microservices without 2PC. A saga is a sequence of local transactions, each publishing an event to trigger the next. Compensating transactions handle failures. Choreography (event-driven) vs Orchestration (central coordinator).
Distributed txCompensation
📬
Outbox Pattern
Guarantee at-least-once event publishing. Instead of publishing events directly to a broker (which can fail after DB write), write events to an "outbox" table in the same DB transaction, then have a separate process publish them to the broker.
ReliabilityAt-least-once
🔌
Strangler Fig
Incrementally replace a legacy system by routing traffic to the new system for specific features while the legacy handles the rest. Over time, the new system "strangles" the old one until it can be retired. Safe, reversible migration strategy.
MigrationZero downtime
🛡️
Anti-Corruption Layer
Translate between two different domain models. When integrating with a legacy system or external service that uses a different language/model, the ACL translates their model to your clean domain model. Prevents external chaos from leaking into your core.
IntegrationDomain isolation
🚥
Sidecar / Ambassador
Attach infrastructure concerns to a service as a co-process. The sidecar runs alongside the main service container and handles cross-cutting concerns: logging, metrics, proxy, mTLS. This is how Istio and Envoy work.
KubernetesService Mesh
⚡
BFF — Backend for Frontend
Dedicated backend per client type. Instead of one API serving mobile, web, and third parties (with conflicting requirements), create a thin BFF per client that aggregates and transforms backend services specifically for that client's needs.
API designMobile vs Web

🌐 Distributed Systems — The Hard Problems

⚠️
The fallacies of distributed computing: The network is reliable. Latency is zero. Bandwidth is infinite. The network is secure. Topology doesn't change. There is one administrator. Transport cost is zero. The network is homogeneous. All false. Plan for all of them.

Key Distributed Systems Challenges Every Architect Must Solve

ProblemSymptomsSolutions
Partial Failure One service succeeds, another fails mid-transaction. State inconsistency. Saga + compensating transactions, Outbox pattern, idempotency keys
Clock Skew Two servers record the same event with different timestamps → wrong ordering Logical clocks (Lamport), vector clocks, NTP with bounded drift tolerance
Split Brain Network partition → two nodes both think they're the leader → conflicting writes Leader election with majority quorum, fencing tokens, ETCD/ZooKeeper
Cascading Failures One slow service causes queue buildup, which overwhelms another service Circuit breakers (Resilience4j), bulkheads (thread pool isolation), timeouts
Thundering Herd Cache expires → 1000 requests hit DB simultaneously Cache stampede protection: mutex lock, probabilistic early expiry, background refresh
Duplicate Processing Message broker retries cause same event processed twice Idempotency keys, deduplication tables, exactly-once Kafka semantics
Data Consistency Read your own writes, monotonic reads, causality — all hard across services Read-after-write: read from leader. Causality: version vectors. Strong: sync replication

Resilience Patterns — Make Your System Fault-Tolerant

ConceptCircuit Breaker States
  CLOSED (normal) → too many failures → OPEN (reject requests, fast-fail)
  OPEN → after cooldown period → HALF-OPEN (let one request through)
  HALF-OPEN → success → CLOSED (back to normal)
  HALF-OPEN → failure → OPEN again

  Configuration: failureThreshold=5, waitDuration=30s, permittedCallsInHalfOpen=3
  Java: Resilience4j CircuitBreaker @CircuitBreaker(name="payment")
  
  Why it helps: Instead of waiting 30s for a timeout × 1000 requests,
  the OPEN state immediately returns an error in <1ms, preventing thread exhaustion.
PatternProblem It SolvesHow
Circuit BreakerSlow/failing downstream cascading upAfter N failures, fail fast for T seconds
Retry with BackoffTransient failures (network blip)Retry 3× with exponential backoff + jitter
BulkheadOne integration exhausting all threadsSeparate thread pool per integration
TimeoutSlow dependency blocking threadsEvery outbound call gets a hard time limit
FallbackService unavailable but partial response possibleReturn cached/default data when primary fails
Rate LimiterProtect service from being overwhelmedToken bucket / sliding window per client

🔧 Design Principles — Beyond SOLID

Coupling & Cohesion — The Physicist's View of Software

Everything in software design is ultimately an attempt to maximize cohesion within modules and minimize coupling between them. These two forces are in constant tension.

Type of CouplingDescriptionRisk Level
ContentOne module directly modifies another's internal dataWorst
Common / GlobalTwo modules share global state (static variables, global DB connection)Very Bad
ControlOne module passes flags to control another's behaviorBad
Stamp / Data StructureModules share a complex data structure even if they only need part of itModerate
DataModules share only the data they actually need (method parameters)OK
MessageModules communicate via events/messages — no direct knowledgeBest

Clean Architecture (Uncle Bob) — Dependency Rule

ArchitectureClean Architecture Layers
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │            Frameworks & Drivers                 │  ← Web, DB, UI, Devices
  │  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐    │
  │  │         Interface Adapters              │    │  ← Controllers, Gateways, Presenters
  │  │  ┌──────────────────────────────────┐   │    │
  │  │  │      Application Use Cases       │   │    │  ← Business rules of the app
  │  │  │  ┌───────────────────────────┐   │   │    │
  │  │  │  │     Enterprise Entities   │   │   │    │  ← Core business objects (pure Java)
  │  │  │  └───────────────────────────┘   │   │    │
  │  │  └──────────────────────────────────┘   │    │
  │  └─────────────────────────────────────────┘    │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
  
  THE DEPENDENCY RULE: Dependencies always point inward.
  Outer layers know about inner layers. Inner layers know NOTHING about outer layers.
  The Entity layer has ZERO imports from Spring, JPA, HTTP, or anything outer.
🎯
Why this matters: Your business logic (the most valuable, most stable part) is completely insulated from technology choices (the most volatile part). You can swap Spring for Quarkus, PostgreSQL for MongoDB, REST for gRPC — without touching a single entity or use case class.

🗺️ Domain-Driven Design (DDD)

💡
DDD is not a technology — it's a way of thinking about software in terms of the business problem. Its value is in creating a shared language between developers and domain experts that makes the code self-documenting and the design naturally aligned with the business.

Strategic DDD — The Big Picture

ConceptSimple ExplanationReal Example
Ubiquitous LanguageA shared vocabulary used by both the code and the business team. No translation layer between "what business says" and "what code says".If finance says "invoice", your class is Invoice, not BillingDocument
Bounded ContextA boundary within which a domain model is consistent. The same word can mean different things in different contexts — that's OK."Customer" in Ordering means someone who buys. In Support it means someone with a ticket. Two different models.
Context MapA diagram showing how bounded contexts relate to each other and integrate.Ordering context → (downstream) → Fulfillment context. Fulfillment context → (ACL) → Legacy Warehouse System
Core DomainThe part of your domain that gives you competitive advantage. Where you invest the most in DDD sophistication.For Spotify: music recommendation. NOT: user authentication (generic).
Subdomain TypesCore (competitive advantage), Supporting (enables core, build in-house), Generic (buy/outsource, not differentiating)Netflix: Core=recommendation engine, Supporting=content management, Generic=email sending

Tactical DDD — Building Blocks in Code

JavaDDD Building Blocks — Practical Examples
// ── VALUE OBJECT: no identity, defined by its attributes, immutable ─
public record EmailAddress(String value) {
    public EmailAddress {
        if (!value.matches("^[\\w.-]+@[\\w.-]+\\.\\w+$"))
            throw new IllegalArgumentException("Invalid email: " + value);
    }
}
// Two EmailAddress("x@y.com") are equal — no separate ID needed

// ── ENTITY: has identity, state can change over lifetime ─────────
public class Customer {
    private final CustomerId id;        // identity is what matters
    private EmailAddress email;          // state can change
    private CustomerStatus status;

    public void changeEmail(EmailAddress newEmail) {
        if (status == CustomerStatus.SUSPENDED)
            throw new DomainException("Suspended customers cannot change email");
        this.email = newEmail;              // domain rule enforced here, not in service
        registerEvent(new CustomerEmailChangedEvent(id, newEmail));
    }
}

// ── AGGREGATE ROOT: entry point that enforces all invariants ─────
public class Order {  // Aggregate root — owns OrderLines
    private final OrderId             id;
    private final List<OrderLine>     lines = new ArrayList<>();
    private OrderStatus               status;
    private static final int           MAX_LINES = 50;

    public void addLine(ProductId product, int qty, Money unitPrice) {
        if (status != OrderStatus.DRAFT)
            throw new DomainException("Can only add lines to draft orders");
        if (lines.size() >= MAX_LINES)
            throw new DomainException("Order cannot exceed 50 lines");
        lines.add(new OrderLine(product, qty, unitPrice));
    }
    // External code NEVER creates OrderLine directly — must go through Order aggregate
}

Database Per Service — The Golden Rule

Each microservice must own its own database and schema. No sharing tables or schemas across service boundaries. This is what enables independent deployment.

❌ Shared DB Anti-Pattern
  • Service A and B both read/write the same table
  • Schema change in one service breaks the other
  • Can't deploy independently
  • Can't scale each service's DB independently
  • Integration via DB = worst coupling possible
✅ DB Per Service
  • Each service owns its tables; no other service touches them
  • Schema evolution contained within one service
  • Each service can use a different DB type (SQL, NoSQL, graph)
  • Cross-service joins done via API calls or event-driven sync
  • True independent deployability

🔒 Security Architecture

Zero Trust Architecture

"Never trust, always verify. Treat every request as if it comes from an untrusted network — regardless of where it originates."

🔐
Verify Explicitly
Always authenticate and authorize based on all available data points: identity, location, device health, service, workload, data classification, and anomalies.
🎯
Least Privilege Access
Limit user and service access with just-in-time (JIT) and just-enough-access (JEA). Minimize blast radius when a credential is compromised.
💥
Assume Breach
Design assuming the attacker is already inside the network. Minimize blast radius, segment access, encrypt everything end-to-end, detect anomalies with behavioral analytics.

Security at Every Layer — Defense in Depth

LayerSecurity Controls
Edge / NetworkWAF (Web Application Firewall), DDoS protection, rate limiting, IP allowlisting
API GatewayAuthentication (JWT/OAuth2), authorization checks, request validation, TLS termination
ApplicationInput validation, output encoding (prevent XSS), CSRF protection, parameterized queries (prevent SQLi)
Service-to-ServicemTLS (mutual TLS), service accounts, signed JWTs, Kubernetes RBAC
DataEncryption at rest (AES-256), encryption in transit (TLS 1.3), field-level encryption for PII
IdentityMFA, short-lived tokens, secret rotation (Vault/AWS Secrets Manager), no hardcoded credentials
ObservabilityAudit logging, SIEM integration, anomaly detection, incident response playbooks

Auth Patterns — OAuth2, OIDC, JWT

FlowOAuth2 Authorization Code Flow (most secure for web apps)
  User → [Click Login] → Your App
  Your App → [Redirect] → Identity Provider (Google/Auth0/Keycloak)
  User → [Enters credentials] → Identity Provider
  Identity Provider → [Authorization Code] → Your App (redirect_uri)
  Your App → [Code + Client Secret] → Identity Provider Token Endpoint
  Identity Provider → [Access Token + ID Token + Refresh Token] → Your App

  Access Token (JWT): short-lived (15m), used for API calls
  ID Token (JWT): contains user identity claims
  Refresh Token: long-lived, used to get new access tokens silently

  JWT Anatomy:
  Header: {"alg":"RS256","typ":"JWT"}
  Payload: {"sub":"user123","email":"x@y.com","roles":["admin"],"exp":1735000000}
  Signature: RSA signature using Identity Provider's private key → your app verifies with public key

Cloud-Native Design Principles (12-Factor App + Extensions)

#FactorWhat It Means
1CodebaseOne codebase tracked in version control. Many deploys. No snowflake deployments.
2DependenciesExplicitly declare and isolate dependencies. Never rely on system-installed packages.
3ConfigStore config in environment variables. Never hardcode URLs, passwords, or environment-specific values in code.
4Backing ServicesTreat databases, queues, and caches as attached resources. Swap them via config, not code changes.
5Build/Release/RunStrictly separate build (compile), release (build + config), and run (execute) stages.
6ProcessesExecute the app as stateless processes. State lives in backing services (DB, cache), not in memory.
7Port BindingExport services via port binding — app is self-contained. No deploying into an app server.
8ConcurrencyScale out via the process model. Add more processes, not bigger machines (horizontal scale).
9DisposabilityFast startup, graceful shutdown. Apps should be killed/restarted at any time without data loss.
10Dev/Prod ParityKeep development, staging, and production as similar as possible. Reduce "works on my machine" bugs.
11LogsTreat logs as event streams. Write to stdout; let the environment collect and aggregate them.
12Admin ProcessesRun admin/management tasks as one-off processes (database migrations, one-time scripts).

SLI / SLO / SLA — The Language of Reliability

ConceptSLI → SLO → SLA hierarchy
  SLI (Service Level Indicator) = a measurable metric that reflects service health
    Examples: request success rate %, P99 latency ms, error rate %, uptime %

  SLO (Service Level Objective) = your internal reliability target
    Examples: "99.9% of requests succeed" | "P99 latency < 200ms over rolling 30 days"
    The SLO is tighter than the SLA — it's your internal commitment to your team.

  SLA (Service Level Agreement) = external contract with customers
    Examples: "We guarantee 99.9% availability, or we refund 10% of monthly fee"
    The SLA is looser than the SLO — it's the floor, not the target.

  ERROR BUDGET = 1 - SLO availability
    99.9% SLO = 0.1% error budget = 43.2 minutes/month of allowed downtime
    If error budget is exhausted → feature work stops → focus on reliability
    If error budget is healthy → can take more deployment risk

GenAI for Architects

Generative AI is now a first-class architectural concern. Every architect must understand how LLMs work, how to build with them, and how to deploy them reliably at scale.

🤖 GenAI Foundations — How It Actually Works

💡
You don't need to be an ML researcher to architect GenAI systems. But you do need to understand the core mechanics well enough to make good architectural decisions about context, latency, cost, reliability, and safety.

How Large Language Models Work — The Mental Model

🔤
Tokenization
Text is split into "tokens" (roughly 3/4 of a word on average). "Hello world" = 2 tokens. "antidisestablishmentarianism" = several. Models think in tokens, not words. Costs are priced per token.
📐
Embeddings
Each token becomes a vector (array of ~1536 numbers). Similar concepts have similar vectors. This is how the model "understands" meaning — meaning is spatial proximity in vector space.
👁️
Attention Mechanism
The "Transformer" in "GPT". For each token, attention calculates which other tokens in the context are most relevant. This is how the model understands "it" refers to "the cat" 20 tokens earlier.
🎲
Next Token Prediction
LLMs are trained to predict the most likely next token given all previous tokens. Generation is sampling — the model outputs a probability distribution, and we pick from it (temperature controls randomness).
🪟
Context Window
The maximum amount of text the model can "see" at once. GPT-4 = 128K tokens (~100K words). The model only knows what's in its context window — it has no memory between conversations by default.
🌡️
Temperature
Controls randomness. Temperature=0: always picks the most likely token (deterministic, factual tasks). Temperature=1: samples proportionally (creative tasks). Temperature=2: wild and unpredictable.

LLM Capability Map — What They're Good At and What They're Not

✅ Strong Capabilities
  • Text generation, summarization, rewriting
  • Code generation and explanation
  • Classification and entity extraction
  • Translation across languages
  • Question answering over provided context
  • Reasoning through structured problems step-by-step
  • Generating multiple options / brainstorming
  • Structured output (JSON, tables) from instructions
❌ Fundamental Limitations
  • Hallucination — confident wrong answers
  • Knowledge cutoff — no real-time information
  • Arithmetic and complex math (use code interpreter)
  • Reliable precise counting and exact recall
  • Consistency over very long context
  • Knowing what it doesn't know
  • Following complex multi-step instructions reliably
  • Safe behavior under adversarial inputs (prompt injection)

🏗️ LLM Integration Architecture

Ways to Use LLMs — From Simple to Complex

1
Direct Prompting: Call the LLM API with a prompt, get a response. Zero infrastructure. Sufficient for simple generation tasks like summarization, classification, drafting. Start here.
2
Prompt Engineering: Craft system prompts that consistently produce the output format you need. Use few-shot examples (2–5 examples in the prompt). Use chain-of-thought ("think step by step"). Most value for least effort.
3
RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation): Inject relevant context into the prompt at runtime. Solves knowledge cutoff and domain-specific knowledge gaps. Required for any production Q&A system over private data. See dedicated RAG section.
4
Function Calling / Tool Use: LLM decides which tools to call (search, calculator, DB query, API). Model returns structured JSON describing the tool call; your code executes it. Bridges LLM reasoning with real-world actions.
5
Fine-Tuning: Train the model further on your domain data to change its behavior or style. Expensive, slower iteration, requires evaluation pipeline. Use only when prompt engineering + RAG have been exhausted and you have thousands of high-quality examples.
6
Agentic Systems: LLM autonomously decides sequences of actions across multiple tools to accomplish a goal. Highest capability, highest complexity, highest failure surface. See dedicated section.

Prompt Engineering — The Architect's Toolkit

PromptHigh-Quality System Prompt Structure
  A well-structured system prompt has 5 components:

  1. ROLE: "You are an expert Java architect reviewing pull requests."
     → Sets the model's persona and knowledge domain

  2. CONTEXT: "You are reviewing a microservice in our e-commerce platform.
               The service handles order processing with Kafka for async events."
     → Provides domain context the model needs to reason correctly

  3. TASK: "Review the provided code for: correctness, performance, security,
            and alignment with our microservices principles."
     → Clear, specific instructions

  4. FORMAT: "Respond as a JSON object: {severity: high|medium|low, category: ...,
              issue: ..., suggestion: ..., code_example: ...}"
     → Structured output is machine-parseable and consistent

  5. CONSTRAINTS: "Do not suggest rewriting the entire service. Focus on the
                   specific code provided. If code is correct, say so."
     → Prevent unwanted behaviors and scope creep

  Advanced techniques:
  - Chain of Thought: "Think step by step before answering"
  - Self-Consistency: Generate 3 answers, pick the majority
  - ReAct: Reason → Act → Observe → repeat (for agents)
  - Few-Shot: Provide 3 example input/output pairs before the real input

📚 RAG — Retrieval-Augmented Generation

💡
RAG solves the core LLM problem: LLMs don't know your private data, and their training data has a cutoff date. RAG lets you inject relevant up-to-date information into every LLM call at runtime — making the LLM knowledgeable about your domain without fine-tuning.

RAG Architecture — Step by Step

ArchitectureRAG Pipeline — Indexing + Retrieval
  ═══ INDEXING PHASE (offline, run once or periodically) ═══════════════
  
  1. LOAD: Ingest documents (PDFs, Confluence, Notion, code, databases)
  2. CHUNK: Split documents into chunks (512–1024 tokens)
     → Overlap chunks by 100 tokens to avoid cutting context at boundaries
  3. EMBED: Convert each chunk to a vector using an embedding model
     → text-embedding-3-large (OpenAI) or all-MiniLM-L6 (local, fast)
  4. STORE: Save (chunk_text, vector, metadata) into a Vector Database
     → Pinecone, Weaviate, Qdrant, pgvector (PostgreSQL extension)

  ═══ RETRIEVAL PHASE (online, per user query) ══════════════════════════
  
  User Query: "How do I configure retry logic in our payment service?"
  
  1. EMBED QUERY: Convert query to vector using the same embedding model
  2. SEARCH: Find top-K most similar chunks in vector DB (cosine similarity)
     → Returns 3–5 most relevant text chunks
  3. RERANK (optional): Use a cross-encoder to reorder chunks by relevance
  4. AUGMENT: Build prompt: system_prompt + retrieved_chunks + user_query
  5. GENERATE: Send augmented prompt to LLM → grounded, accurate response
  6. CITE: Include source references in the response for trust + verification

RAG Quality Improvements — What Architects Tune

ProblemRoot CauseSolution
Wrong chunks retrievedChunks too large; query and document use different vocabularySmaller chunks, HyDE (generate hypothetical answer, embed it), hybrid search (BM25 + vector)
Context lost at chunk boundariesSplitting mid-sentence or mid-conceptSemantic chunking (split at section boundaries), parent-child chunking
Irrelevant chunks retrievedEmbedding model too genericFine-tune embedding model on domain data, add metadata filters to retrieval
LLM ignores retrieved contextContext buried in the middle of a long prompt"Lost in the middle" problem — put most relevant context at start or end of prompt
Stale informationIndex not updatedIncremental indexing pipeline, document change detection, TTL-based re-indexing
Hallucination in responsesLLM generates from training, not from contextGrounding instructions ("Only use the provided context"), citation enforcement

Advanced RAG Patterns

🔀
Hybrid Search
Combine vector similarity search (semantic) with BM25 keyword search (lexical). Merge results with Reciprocal Rank Fusion. Beats either alone for most use cases.
🌳
RAPTOR / Hierarchical
Summarize clusters of chunks into higher-level summaries. Build a tree. Search at multiple levels. Answers both "what is X?" (detailed) and "summarize the whole thing" (high-level).
🔄
Self-RAG
LLM decides whether retrieval is needed at all. If yes, it retrieves, then critiques whether retrieved docs are relevant. Reduces unnecessary retrieval and low-quality augmentation.
🔮
HyDE
Hypothetical Document Embeddings. Generate a hypothetical answer to the query, embed it, use it to search. The hypothetical answer's vocabulary matches the document vocabulary better than the raw question.
🔍
Multi-Query
Generate 3–5 variations of the user's question, retrieve for each, deduplicate. Increases recall for queries that can be asked multiple ways.
💾
Contextual Retrieval
Anthropic's technique: before chunking, have an LLM prepend a short context sentence to each chunk ("This section describes the retry configuration for the payment service"). Dramatically improves chunk relevance.

🤖 Agentic Systems — LLMs That Take Actions

⚠️
Agentic systems are the most powerful and the most risky GenAI architecture. An LLM making autonomous multi-step decisions can accomplish remarkable things — and cause remarkable damage. Architect with strong guardrails, human-in-the-loop checkpoints, and blast-radius minimization.

The ReAct Loop — How Agents Work

PatternReAct: Reasoning + Acting Loop
  User: "Find all orders placed by customer #123 last month, check if any 
         are delayed, and send them a personalized apology email."

  Iteration 1:
    Thought: "I need to find orders for customer #123 from last month."
    Action: query_orders(customer_id=123, start=2025-05-01, end=2025-05-31)
    Observation: [{id:1001, status:DELAYED}, {id:1002, status:DELIVERED}]

  Iteration 2:
    Thought: "Order 1001 is delayed. I need details to personalize the email."
    Action: get_order_details(order_id=1001)
    Observation: {product:"Laptop", expected:2025-05-10, actual:null, reason:"supply"}

  Iteration 3:
    Thought: "I have enough context. I'll draft a personalized apology email."
    Action: generate_email(customer=123, order=1001, reason="supply chain delay")
    Observation: "Dear Sarah, We're sorry your laptop order..."

  Iteration 4:
    Thought: "Email looks good. I should send it."
    Action: ⚠️ PAUSE → HUMAN APPROVAL (before sending)
    [Human approves] → send_email(to="sarah@...", body="Dear Sarah...")

  Final Answer: "Sent apology email for order 1001. Order 1002 was on time."

Orchestrator + Subagents One LLM plans and coordinates; specialized subagents execute specific tasks Complex workflows with distinct subtasks (research + write + review)
Pipeline (Waterfall) Sequential: agent A's output feeds agent B, feeds agent C Predictable, linear transformations (extract → enrich → format)
Parallelization Multiple agents work on different parts simultaneously; results merged Independent subtasks that benefit from parallel execution (evaluate 5 options)

🧩 GenAI Design Patterns

🔗
Prompt Chaining
Break complex tasks into a sequence of LLM calls. Output of call N becomes input to call N+1. Each call has a focused, simple task. More reliable than one mega-prompt.
🚦
Routing / Classifier
First LLM call classifies the input into categories. Each category routes to a different specialized prompt or pipeline. E.g., classify as "technical question" vs "billing question" → different handlers.
🌀
Parallelization
Run multiple LLM calls simultaneously on different aspects of a task. Aggregate results. Faster than sequential chains. Example: evaluate code for correctness, security, and style in parallel.
🔁
Self-Correction Loop
Generate → Validate (programmatically or with another LLM call) → if invalid, provide error feedback and regenerate. Especially useful for structured output (JSON, SQL, code) that must pass validation.
📝
Structured Output
Use function calling / JSON mode to force LLM output into a defined schema. Parse it programmatically. Never regex-parse free-form LLM text in production — use structured output APIs.
🛡️
Input/Output Guardrails
Validate inputs for prompt injection, PII, off-topic requests. Validate outputs for hallucination markers, policy violations, quality thresholds. Nemo Guardrails, custom classifiers, rule-based filters.
🧪
LLM-as-Judge
Use a separate LLM call to evaluate the quality of another LLM's output. Scales QA without human annotation. Use a stronger model as judge, weaker model as generator. Define clear evaluation rubric.
💰
Cost-Aware Routing
Route simple queries to cheap/fast models (GPT-4o-mini, Claude Haiku), complex queries to expensive/capable models (GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet). 80% of queries are simple — 10× cost savings possible.

LLMOps — Operating LLM Systems

📊
Evaluation
Build offline eval datasets. Score responses on: faithfulness, relevance, coherence, groundedness. Run evals on every prompt change and model upgrade. Tools: RAGAS, PromptFoo, LangSmith.
📈
Observability
Log every LLM call: prompt (with params), response, latency, tokens (input/output/total), cost, model version, user ID. Query logs to debug quality issues. Tools: LangSmith, Helicone, Arize.
🔄
Prompt Versioning
Treat prompts as code. Version control them. A/B test prompt changes before full rollout. Never change a production prompt without testing it on your eval dataset first.
💰
Cost Management
Track cost per user, per feature, per request type. Set budget alerts. Use semantic caching (exact or similar prompts → serve cached response). Route to smaller models where quality allows.
🧪
Red Teaming
Adversarially test your system for prompt injection, jailbreaks, PII leakage, toxic output. Do this before launch, not after an incident. Automate red-team tests in CI.
🔁
Feedback Loops
Collect thumbs up/down signals from users. Build a human feedback pipeline. Use good/bad examples to improve prompts and fine-tune future models. Continuous improvement is the moat.

🎯 Decision Making & Architecture Decision Records

ADR — Architecture Decision Record Template

An ADR documents a single significant architectural decision — the context, the decision, and the consequences. They're the most valuable documentation an architect can produce.

MarkdownADR-0001: Use Kafka for Async Service Communication
  # ADR-0001: Use Apache Kafka for Asynchronous Inter-Service Communication
  
  ## Status
  Accepted | Date: 2025-09-15 | Author: @arch-team
  Supersedes: ADR-0000 (RabbitMQ decision, 2024)
  
  ## Context
  Our order processing involves 7 microservices that need to react to OrderPlaced
  events: inventory, shipping, email, analytics, fraud, rewards, finance.
  We need to:
  - Fan-out one event to many consumers independently
  - Replay events for new consumers being onboarded
  - Handle 50,000 events/sec at peak (Black Friday)
  - Maintain event ordering within a single order's lifecycle
  
  ## Decision
  Use Apache Kafka (MSK on AWS) as our primary async messaging infrastructure.
  
  ## Rationale (Options Considered)
  1. RabbitMQ: Good for queues, poor for fan-out replay, doesn't scale to 50K/sec easily
  2. AWS SQS+SNS: Managed, but no log replay, harder ordering guarantees
  3. ✅ Kafka: Log-based, fan-out native, replay built-in, 50K+/sec, partition ordering
  
  ## Consequences
  + Event replay: onboard new consumers without re-processing source systems
  + Audit log: Kafka topic is an immutable event log for free
  + Scale: handles 10x our current peak
  - Operational complexity: requires MSK cluster management, monitoring
  - Learning curve: team unfamiliar with Kafka consumer group semantics
  - Eventual consistency: consumers may be behind; downstream reads may be stale
  
  ## Action Items
  - [ ] Platform team: provision MSK cluster with 3-AZ replication
  - [ ] All teams: add Kafka consumer dead-letter queue + retry logic
  - [ ] Runbook: Kafka consumer lag alerting (alert if lag > 10,000 messages)

The Architect's Decision Framework

1
Define the problem clearly. What are we actually trying to solve? What are the constraints (time, cost, team skill, existing system)? What does success look like? A well-framed problem often reveals the answer.
2
Identify the quality attributes in tension. Performance vs Cost? Consistency vs Availability? Simplicity vs Flexibility? Name the trade-off explicitly. Most architectural decisions are choosing which attribute to prioritize.
3
Generate 2–3 real alternatives. Never evaluate a single option — you'll always find reasons it works. Force yourself to have at least 2 credible alternatives. "Use X" vs "Use Y" vs "Build our own".
4
Evaluate against your specific context. Team size, existing skill set, scale requirements, timeline, operational maturity. The same pattern can be correct in one context and catastrophic in another.
5
Decide and document. Pick an option and write an ADR. State the decision, the rationale, and the consequences (including downsides). "We chose X because [specific reasons]. The trade-off is [acknowledged cost]."
6
Revisit with new information. Decisions are not permanent. Mark ADRs as "Superseded" when better options emerge. The goal is not to be right forever — it's to make the best decision with current information and update it as you learn.

The Architecture Presentation — C4 Model

💡
The C4 Model (by Simon Brown) gives architects a standard way to present architecture at 4 levels of zoom. Use the right level for the right audience — non-technical stakeholders need Level 1; developers need Levels 3–4.
LevelNameShowsAudience
L1 System Context Your system as a single box. What external systems and users interact with it. Everyone — CEOs, product, sales
L2 Container The major deployable units inside your system (web app, API, database, cache). Technical stakeholders, architects
L3 Component The major components inside one container (controllers, services, repositories). Development team
L4 Code Class/module level. Auto-generate from code — don't maintain manually. Developers (rarely needed)

🗺️ Technical Roadmaps

How to Build a Technical Roadmap That Actually Gets Funded

1
Baseline the current state. Document your current architecture, pain points, incidents from last 6 months, tech debt backlog, team velocity data. Make the pain concrete and quantifiable.
2
Define the target state. What does the architecture look like in 2 years? Draw it out. Define the quality attributes you're targeting. Be specific: "P99 latency < 100ms at 100K RPS" not "better performance".
3
Map the gap. What's the delta between current and target? Break it into concrete initiatives. Each initiative should have a clear outcome, not just an activity. "Migrate auth to Auth0" not "Improve authentication".
4
Prioritize by impact × effort × risk. Sequence initiatives that unblock other initiatives first. Tackle highest-risk items early (fail fast, not late). Deliver quick wins in month 1–3 to build trust.
5
Connect to business outcomes. Every initiative must have a business case. "Migrating to Kubernetes saves $200K/year in EC2 costs and reduces deploy time from 3h to 15min, enabling 2× deploy frequency."
6
Review and adapt quarterly. A 2-year technical roadmap is a direction, not a plan. Review quarterly. Update based on what you learned. New business priorities change your sequencing. Architectural thinking is continuous.

🏆 The Architect's North Star

Great architects build systems that let organizations move fast, safely, forever. Not just today. Not just at this scale. Not just with the current team. They design for the system to be evolvable — so that the architecture you build today doesn't become the legacy that holds the company back in 5 years. That is the job.

Your daily checklist as an architect:
  1. Did I make at least one decision today with a documented rationale?
  2. Did I explain the why behind at least one decision to a developer?
  3. Did I look at the system from the business perspective, not just the technical one?
  4. Did I reduce complexity somewhere, even by a little?
  5. Did I ask: "what happens when this fails?" about at least one component?
